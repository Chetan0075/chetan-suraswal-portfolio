How I Think

Decisions First.
Dashboards Second.

This page isn't a personality summary. It's the actual sequence I run every analysis through, and the assumptions I refuse to make along the way.

See It Applied ↗

The Process

Five Steps.
No Shortcuts.

01

Start with the decision, not the dataset.

I don't open a dataset until I know what decision it needs to support, and who's actually making it. If there's no decision on the other end, it's not analysis — it's curiosity.

02

Audit the data before trusting it.

Where did it come from, what's missing, and what assumptions are already baked in? Most bad insights aren't bad analysis — they're bad data wearing a good chart.

03

Model the mechanism, not the trend.

A trend tells you what happened. A model tells you why — and whether it's still going to be true next quarter.

04

Design for the person who has to act, not the dashboard.

If a metric doesn't change what someone does on Monday morning, it doesn't earn a tile — no matter how interesting it looked in the notebook.

05

End with a sentence, not just a chart.

Every analysis closes with a recommendation that starts with "So we should—". If it doesn't, it isn't finished, it's just decorated.

What I Won't Do

Rigor Isn't Optional.

  • I don't smooth over outliers without explaining why they exist.
  • I don't call a trend line "predictive" just because it has a confidence band around it.
  • I don't ship a metric I can't explain in one sentence, out loud, to someone who isn't an analyst.
  • I don't present an assumption as if it were a fact.
  • I don't optimize a dashboard for "looks impressive" over "is accurate."
